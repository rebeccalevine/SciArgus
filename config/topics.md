#Machine Learning and AI
Research on the use of machine learning and AI to advance the study of ecology. This includes methods to study life history questions, reproduction, morphology, physiology, movement, and cognition, as well as novel benchmarks that measure AI capabilities in this field. Additionally, we welcome thought leadership and perspectives on the promises and perils of integrating AI into ecology.

#Nutritional Ecology
Research integrating nutrition with population and quantitative ecology to understand behavior, resource allocation, and life history in large mammals. Work that seeks to reveal the mechanistic underpinnings of individual fitness and population dynamics, with a particular emphasis on how nutritional status interacts with environmental variability, disease, predation, and climate change to shape the trajectories of long-lived species populations.

#Life History and Reproductive Ecology
Studies of reproductive investment, trade-offs, and chronology in large, long-lived animals. Research that explores how animals allocate limited resources between growth, reproduction, and survival across seasons and years, including the effects of body condition, age, and environmental context on reproductive decisions and offspring performance.

#Resource Allocation and Life History Strategies
Investigation of how large mammals balance competing energetic demands across seasons, allocating limited resources among growth, reproduction, and survival. Work that examines how nutritional state and environmental conditions interact to shape life-history decisions, including the non-linear dynamics of fat expenditure over winter and how individual condition translates into fitness outcomes across a long-lived animal's lifetime.

#Thermoregulation and Climate Change
Research on how large mammals, particularly ungulates at the margins of their range, respond behaviorally and physiologically to thermal stress in a warming climate. Work that examines how traits such as sex and reproductive status shape heat sensitivity and thermoregulatory strategies, and investigates the feedback loops between an animal's internal state, its behavior, and the thermal environment — with implications for understanding how climate change will affect wildlife population viability.

#Moose Ecology at Range Margins
Field-based investigation of moose populations occupying the southern extent of their native range, where warming temperatures pose acute conservation challenges. Research that focuses on how moose balance thermoregulatory demands with reproduction and survival, including habitat selection for microclimate refugia, effects of heat stress on rutting behavior, and the demographic consequences — survival, pregnancy rates, and calf recruitment — of living near thermal limits.

#Animal Behavior, Decision-Making, and Individuality
Examination of how individual animals make decisions in a changing world, drawing on fine-scale behavioral data from GPS and accelerometer technology. This includes research on how behavioral states, temperament, and experience-based learning shape responses to predation risk, environmental stress, and resource variability across an animal's lifetime.

#Maternal Effects and Carry-Over Consequences
Research on how the experiences and condition of mothers — particularly during environmentally stressful periods — leave lasting legacies for offspring performance and population dynamics. This includes how harsh winters experienced by pregnant females propagate through time, influencing the development, behavior, and survival of their young well beyond the initial disturbance event.

#Thermal Ecology and Climate Adaptation
Research on how temperature shapes the performance, survival, and fitness of ectotherms, with a particular focus on insects. This work examines how organisms respond to thermal variation across developmental and adult life stages, how thermoregulatory behavior buffers populations from environmental change, and how the thermal sensitivity of survival and reproduction diverge — with consequences for population persistence under climate warming.

#Evolutionary Interactions Between Thermal Biology and Sexual Selection
Theoretical and empirical investigation of the reciprocal feedbacks between an organism's thermal ecology and the traits shaped by sexual selection — including ornaments, weapons, and courtship displays. This research develops and tests hypotheses for how mating system dynamics generate selection on thermal traits, and how the thermal costs of mate competition drive the co-adaptation of thermal physiology and sexually selected characters, in both pre- and post-copulatory contexts.

#Co-adaptation of Sexual Ornaments and Thermal Physiology
Empirical research using dragonflies as a model system to test whether heat-absorbing sexual coloration co-adapts with physiological heat tolerance. This work uses comparative and phylogenetic approaches to evaluate whether the evolution of thermally costly ornaments is constrained or compensated by corresponding changes in thermal physiology, and what this means for ecological niche expansion and vulnerability to extinction.

#Sexual Ornamentation and Extinction Risk
Population- and macroevolutionary-scale research on whether sexual ornamentation buffers or exacerbates species' vulnerability to habitat modification and environmental change. Using long-term occupancy data and phylogenetic comparative methods, this work tests theory predicting that condition-dependent ornamentation can reduce intersexual conflict and accelerate fixation of advantageous alleles, thereby providing ornamented taxa with resilience in novel or stressful environments.

#Thermal Effects on Mating Systems and Reproductive Coordination
Experimental research on how temperature disrupts or facilitates the coordination of mating between males and females with mismatched thermal optima for courtship activity. This includes work on how sex-specific thermal sensitivity in signaling and response behavior can cause temperature-driven breakdowns in mating success, and how these dynamics shape the evolution of mating systems under climate change.

#Carry-Over Effects and Developmental Temperature
Research on how thermal conditions experienced during juvenile development shape adult reproductive performance through carry-over effects. This work investigates the fitness consequences of developmental temperature for adult fertility and reproductive output, and evaluates whether reproductive carry-over effects could buffer populations against the mortality costs of elevated temperatures.

#Eco-Evolutionary Dynamics of Sexually Selected Traits
Broad theoretical and synthetic interest in how ecological context — particularly climate — shapes the evolution of sexually selected traits, and how the evolution of those traits in turn alters organisms' ecological interactions and adaptive trajectories. This includes exploration of how sexual selection can serve as an engine of ecological diversification through the co-optation of mating traits for viability-related functions.

#Physiology, Morphology, and Ecology of Antlers
Research on cervid antlers. This includes the physiology of antlers growth, patterns in morphology or factors influencing morphology, and the ecological role of antlers.
